Why Are My Teeth Loose?

Tooth mobility (loose teeth in adults) is a serious dental condition most commonly caused by advanced periodontal disease that has destroyed the supporting bone around the teeth. Other causes include traumatic injury, teeth grinding (bruxism), bite imbalances, and orthodontic movement. Without treatment, mobile teeth can shift, cause pain while eating, and eventually be lost.

At Dr. Kathuria's Dentistry, we take a comprehensive approach to mobile teeth. First, we address the underlying cause — treating gum disease, adjusting the bite, or managing bruxism. Then, we stabilize the teeth using advanced splinting techniques that bond the loose teeth to their stable neighbours, distributing biting forces evenly and allowing the supporting tissues to heal.

Fibre-reinforced composite splinting (Ribbond/Everstick)
Minimally invasive — no tooth preparation needed

The Splinting Process

1

Comprehensive Assessment

We evaluate mobility grade, bone levels via X-rays, pocket depths, and bite alignment to determine the cause and create a treatment plan.

2

Treat the Root Cause

Periodontal treatment (scaling, root planing, or surgery) is performed first to eliminate gum disease and create a healthy foundation for splinting.

3

Splint Application

A high-strength fibre ribbon is bonded to the inner surfaces of the mobile teeth and their stable neighbours using composite resin, creating a rigid unit.

4

Bite Adjustment & Review

The bite is refined to distribute forces evenly. Regular follow-ups monitor stability, splint integrity, and gum healing over the coming months.

Frequently Asked Questions

In many cases, loose teeth can be saved. The prognosis depends on the amount of remaining bone support and the underlying cause. Teeth with Grade I and Grade II mobility (slight to moderate) often respond well to periodontal treatment combined with splinting. Severely mobile teeth with extensive bone loss (Grade III) may require extraction and replacement with implants.

A well-placed fibre-reinforced composite splint can last 3-5 years or longer with proper oral hygiene and regular dental check-ups. The splint's longevity depends on the quality of the bond, the patient's bite forces, and adherence to maintenance recommendations. The splint can be easily repaired or replaced if needed.

Yes, you can eat normally once the splint is placed. However, we recommend avoiding extremely hard foods (such as ice, hard nuts, or biting into hard fruits) directly on the splinted area. The splint is designed to withstand normal biting and chewing forces while distributing the load across multiple teeth for stability.
